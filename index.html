Its Harder to Access Polling Sites In NYC, Depending On Where You Live

An analysis of the latest census data and NYC polling locations shows that voters may have more difficulty casting their ballots in certain areas.

By: Sadie Brown

November 18, 2021

Hands palcing a ballot into a ballot box via Unsplash

In New York City, democratic ideals like “one person, one vote” may be harder to achieve when access to the polls is uneven based on location. Each borough has a certain number of polling locations, but when compared to the populations of those boroughs, some polling locations wind up serving way more people than others. This puts those locations at risk of being inaccessible due to long lines, wait times, or proximity to public transportation. An analysis of polling locations and 2020 census data showed that the borough of Queens, specifically Jackson Heights, is serving a disproportionate number of voters compared to other boroughs and neighborhoods.

A comparison of neighborhood demographics shows that in the area where more voters are served at each station (Jackson Heights), the majority of the population is nonwhite. In the area where the least voters are served at each station (Coney Island), the majority of the population is white.

For this chart, "eligible voters" were calculated using population over 18.
